Four Problems вЂ” Root Cause → Fix → Outcome

Each issue was treated as a distinct root-cause problem, not a one-off technical fix.

Problem 1 вЂ” Test Visibility

Workers had a dangerously limited view during active testing

Root cause: No external cameras, requiring workers inside the connex box to stop tests for visual checks
Fix: Deployed UniFi camera system with pole-mounted views across the test area
Outcome: Tests no longer needed to stop for visual checks вЂ” safety and continuity improved

Problem 2 вЂ” Remote Observation

Leadership and remote team members had no live view of testing

Root cause: Only one connex box, limiting real-time visibility for leadership and remote team members
Fix: Connected the connex computer system to the camera network so remote observers could watch live
Outcome: Leadership had live visibility without crowding the connex box or waiting for post-test updates

Problem 3 вЂ” Remote Connectivity

Field crews drove to hotels to upload data when cell service failed

Root cause: No failover connectivity in remote areas, with a single weak carrier causing complete upload failures
Fix: Built a multi-SIM Cradlepoint mobile network with a large antenna on the trailer, providing carrier failover and extended signal range
Outcome: Field teams uploaded data on-site, eliminating the need to drive to hotels for connectivity

Problem 4 вЂ” Consumable Parts

Untracked O-rings and consumables caused avoidable R&D downtime

Root cause: No structured inventory system, causing parts to run out without warning and halting work until restocked
Fix: Activated eMaint CMMS (already paid for, unused), set up bins and scanner for check-in/check-out tracking
Outcome: 95% reduction in downtime from parts shortages, with reorder triggers preventing stockouts before they occur

Method

Why This Was Built

The Problem

  • 15+ active contractors managed reactively across texts, emails, and memory
  • No single place to check credential status, contact info, or performance history
  • License and insurance expiration tracked manually, with compliance gaps going unnoticed
  • No standardized SOW, causing scope, risk, and accountability to vary by engagement

  • 12-section SOW template governing scope, risk, and accountability for every engagement prior to mobilization
  • Approval gate that blocks vendor assignment when credentials are expired

4.0 How the System Works

Step 1 — Intake

A single form submission creates both CRM records

The intake flow starts when a vendor submits a Microsoft Form. The flow receives the form data, writes the contact information into the Contact CRM, then writes the company information into the Company CRM. That means a new vendor enters the system fully onboarded from one submission rather than from manual back-and-forth entry.

Step 2 — Record structure

Company data and contact data stay linked, not mixed

The Company CRM stores the operational company record: company type, trade, years in business, status, payment terms, lead time notes, license number, credential dates, trade discount, whether a license is required, and the reminder-sent fields. The Contact CRM stores the people tied to that company, including role and primary contact status, which is what allows the reminder flow to find the right person later.

Step 3 — Pre-work controls

A standardized SOW governs every engagement before mobilization

Before work starts, each contractor engagement is governed by a branded 12-section Statement of Work covering project overview, scope, technical specifications, code compliance, permit responsibility, insurance and liability requirements, scheduling protocol, documentation requirements, change orders, closeout, warranty terms, and signatures. That keeps scope, risk, and accountability explicit before any field work begins.

Step 4 — Post-job evaluation

Completed work is scored and summarized, not left to memory

After each completed job, the vendor is scored across Quality, Schedule, Communication, and Cost using the weighted formula (Quality × 0.40) + (Schedule × 0.30) + (Communication × 0.20) + (Cost × 0.10). The history log captures each work instance, the summary tab rolls the data up by company, and the workbook guide documents the scoring logic so decisions are based on a preserved baseline instead of recency bias or one-off impressions.

Step 5 — Automated enforcement

Expired credentials close the approval gate automatically

Whenever a CRM item is created or modified, the revocation flow checks whether a license is required, whether it has expired, and whether insurance is still current. If either credential fails, the flow moves Approved to Work from Yes to No. The gate closes itself, so compliance does not depend on someone remembering to check dates manually.

Current credentials ГўвЂ вЂ™ Approved to Work = Yes

Step 6 — Automated reminder

The primary contact gets the reminder, and duplicate sends are blocked

A separate flow checks whether a license or insurance credential is expiring within seven days. If so, it queries the Contact CRM, loops through the linked contacts, validates that the primary contact has a usable email address, sends a personalized reminder explaining that the license or insurance is expiring in your system, and asks for the updated policy or license number. It then marks the reminder field as sent so another email is not triggered if the record is edited again before the issue is resolved.

How the Budget Workbook Was Structured

Three cross-linked tabs, each serving a distinct control function.

Tab 1 вЂ” Project Summary Budget

  • Purchase price + net acquisition cost
  • Total renovation budget vs. ARV
  • Projected profit and return on cost
  • Formulas cross-linked to line items

Tab 2 вЂ” PERT Labor Estimates

  • Optimistic, most likely, pessimistic per trade
  • Weighted average labor duration by scope
  • Sequencing dependencies tracked
  • Used for scheduling and vendor follow-through

Tab 3 вЂ” Material Line Items

  • Material quantities, unit costs, line totals
  • Scope sections: siding, electrical, HVAC, windows
  • Estimate vs. actual logic for cost drift detection
  • Subtotals per trade feed summary tab formulas

Foundation Repair & Flatwork Phase

The SOW turns the foundation scope into concrete operating controls instead of a vague contractor promise.

Defined Scope

  • 24 total pier locations across the perimeter and interior repair points
  • 9 tunnel excavation points and 5 concrete perforation points coordinated to reach the repair areas
  • 216 square feet of flatwork demolition and replacement with rebar and dowel pin reinforcement
  • 106 linear feet of formwork and debris removal included in the scope

Execution Controls

  • Sequencing requirement: pier installation had to be complete and verified before flatwork could begin
  • Daily status updates and written communications for approvals, clarifications, and deviations
  • Stage-by-stage photo documentation required before, during, and after installation
  • Written change orders only, plus final survey, punch list, closeout package, and transferable lifetime warranty

How the Workbook and SOW Worked Together

Before Mobilization

The workbook framed the project financially while the SOW locked the foundation scope, contractor responsibilities, and documentation requirements before field work began.

During Execution

Budget assumptions, vendor follow-through, sequencing, and site documentation could be managed together instead of living in separate spreadsheets, emails, and verbal agreements.

At Closeout

The workbook protected margin visibility while the SOW defined the final survey, permits, photos, punch list, and warranty package needed for proper project closeout.
